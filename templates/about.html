How do New Yorkers Commute to Work?

  • In this project, I explore the various ways that New Yorkers commute to New York. My goal is to discover which modes of transportation are commonly used in different boroughs and propose some explanation for the trends the data reflect. Possible explanations may include the available infrastructure, the topography, or the the socio-economic status of people in the region. I also consider the environmental impact of trends that are apparent in the data. My dataset records the number of people in each of the 5 boroughs of NYC who commute to work using public transportation, motor vehicles, bicycles, or by walking. My dataset comes from nyc.gov database of Environment and Health Data.
  • Some important vocabulary terms in this infographic include:
    • Modes of transportation: a method of traveling from one place to the next
    • Public transportation: the subway, public buses, ferries, etc.
    • Motor Vehicles: examples include cars, trucks, vans

Data Cleansing

  1. Delete all rows that do not contain data for 2017-2021 (all rows past row 305)
  2. Within the 2017-2021 data, only keep data whose geotag is either UHF42 or borough or citywide
  3. Delete the GeoID and GeoRank columns
  4. Change column titles so as to remove spaces, parenthesis, commas
  5. Use google sheets “custom number formats” to remove commas in numbers with more than three digits. It is only necessary to apply this command to the “number” columns, and not the “percent” columns(removing these special characters can simplify converting the csv file to the json file)

Prototypes:

  • In my prototype for my macro page, I decided to use a map-based infographic because I think having a pictorial representation of geographic data is a simple, intuitive way of organizing my data that makes sense to viewers. Also, a map-based infographic is particularly helpful for my project because my dataset is about how people travel to work every day and seeing a map immediately puts viewers in the correct headspace to wonder if some of the trends they see exist due to certain geographic challenges that are specific to different regions. On my map, each borough is shaded a different hue of green (as set out in my legend) corresponding to the percent of people who use cars, trucks, and vans to commute to work in that region.
  • In my prototype for my micro page, I used a bar chart to compare the percent of people who use different forms of transportation with those who use cars, trucks, and vans and included some qualitative analysis below the graph to summarize the trends for a specific borough. Although, I am using percentages as my metric, I decided to use a bar chart rather than a pie chart because the percentages in my data to do not add up to 100% and I also think using a bar chart makes differences between amounts that are close to each other easier to identify.
  • The main points of feedback two classmates offered after looking at my prototypes were that I could provide more a detailed analysis of the trends in each borough beneath the graph on my micro page and also that I might consider using a pie chart instead of a bar graph. Moving forward, I think I will include a more specific analysis on my micro page, but I think I will stick with a bar graph primarily because the percentages for each borough do not sum to 100 unfortunately, and I am not sure what the missing percent indicates.
